\begin{abstract}
Music generation represents a significant challenge in artificial intelligence due to the complex, hierarchical, and temporal nature of musical structure. Traditional supervised learning approaches are constrained by the high cost and scarcity of annotated data. This project explores a progressive roadmap of unsupervised deep learning architectures to generate multi-genre music. We implement a four-stage pipeline: (1) an autoregressive LSTM Autoencoder for single-genre reconstruction, (2) a Variational Autoencoder (VAE) featuring bidirectional encoders and $\beta$-annealing to capture multi-genre diversity, (3) a Causal Music Transformer utilizing event-based tokenization for long-horizon coherence, and (4) Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) via a custom reward model to align generative outputs with human aesthetic preferences. Evaluated on the MAESTRO and Lakh MIDI datasets, our progressive methodology demonstrates substantial improvements across tasks. The final RLHF-tuned Transformer achieved a human listening score of 4.8/5.0 and a perplexity of 8.73, significantly outperforming naive random and Markov Chain baselines in structural coherence and rhythmic diversity.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Music is an intricate temporal signal characterized by highly structured patterns, including melody progression, harmonic tension, and genre-specific rhythmic variations. While deep learning has revolutionized natural language processing and computer vision, music generation presents unique challenges: the data is highly dimensional, polyphonic (multiple notes occurring simultaneously), and exhibits dependencies across extremely long time horizons. 

Traditional supervised approaches to music generation require extensive labeled datasets, which are prohibitively expensive to curate. Consequently, this study focuses on unsupervised generative neural networks capable of learning rich musical representations directly from raw, unannotated MIDI data. 

The primary objective of this project is to build a robust deep learning framework capable of generating novel, high-fidelity music pieces across diverse genres such as Classical, Jazz, Rock, and Pop. To achieve this, we follow a progressive four-task methodology, advancing from basic sequence reconstruction to advanced reinforcement learning techniques guided by human feedback.

\section{Related Work}
Deep learning for symbolic music generation has evolved significantly. Early approaches utilized Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks to predict sequential note events. However, standard LSTMs struggle with long-term structural coherence. 

To address diversity and continuous latent space mapping, Roberts et al. introduced MusicVAE, which demonstrated that hierarchical decoders could interpolate between musical sequences effectively. More recently, self-attention mechanisms and Transformers (Vaswani et al.) have become the state-of-the-art for sequence modeling. The ``Music Transformer'' utilized relative positional attention to handle extreme sequence lengths. Finally, inspired by the success of InstructGPT and LLMs, adapting Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) to symbolic music allows models to optimize for subjective human aesthetics rather than mere statistical likelihood.

\section{Problem Definition}
Let a music sequence be represented as a discrete time series:
\begin{equation}
X=\{x_{1},x_{2},...,x_{T}\}
\end{equation}
where each $x_{t}$ is a symbolic event such as a note-on, note-off, velocity change, or duration shift. Our objective is to learn an unsupervised generative distribution $p_{\theta}(X)$ such that the model can sample novel sequences $\hat{X}\sim p_{\theta}(X)$ to generate realistic, genre-consistent music.

\section{Dataset and Preprocessing}
Data representation is a critical bottleneck in music generation. We utilized two distinct datasets and representation formats depending on the task architecture.

\subsection{Datasets}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{MAESTRO Dataset:} Used for Task 1 to provide a stable, single-genre (Classical Piano) baseline.
    \item \textbf{Lakh MIDI Dataset (LMD):} Used for Tasks 2, 3, and 4 to provide a massive, multi-genre collection for learning diverse latent spaces.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Piano-Roll Matrix Representation (Tasks 1 \& 2)}
For the LSTM and VAE models, MIDI files were parsed into dense piano-roll matrices. We normalized the timing resolution and clipped the pitch range to 88 keys (standard piano). To prevent the models from memorizing silence, we implemented a density filter, discarding any sequence segment containing fewer than 15 active note events.

\subsection{Event-Based Tokenization (Tasks 3 \& 4)}
Dense matrices are inefficient for Transformers due to extreme sparsity. We engineered a custom \texttt{MusicTokenizer} to convert MIDI into a 1D stream of vocabulary events:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \texttt{NOTE\_ON\_p} and \texttt{NOTE\_OFF\_p} ($p \in [0, 127]$).
    \item \texttt{TIME\_SHIFT\_t}: Quantized the delta time between notes into 10ms bins (up to 1.0 second).
    \item \texttt{VELOCITY\_v}: Mapped 128 MIDI velocities into 32 quantized volume bins.
\end{itemize}
This dynamic vocabulary resulted in exactly 392 discrete tokens, allowing the Transformer to process music identically to natural language.

\section{Methodology and Architectures}

\subsection{Task 1: LSTM Autoencoder}
The baseline model is an autoregressive LSTM Autoencoder designed for single-genre reconstruction. The encoder learns a latent embedding $z=f_{\phi}(X)$, and the decoder reconstructs $\hat{X}=g_{\theta}(z)$. 

\textbf{Architecture details:} We utilized an input dimension of 88, a hidden dimension of 256, and a compressed latent dimension of 128. During training, we utilized a Teacher Forcing ratio of 50\% to stabilize the autoregressive decoding process. The objective was to minimize the Binary Cross Entropy (BCE) loss:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{AE}=\sum_{t=1}^{T}||x_{t}-\hat{x}_{t}||^{2}
\end{equation}



\subsection{Task 2: Variational Autoencoder (VAE)}
To generate diverse music across multiple genres, we extended the Autoencoder into a VAE by enforcing a Gaussian prior on the latent space. 

\textbf{Architecture details:} The encoder was upgraded to a Bidirectional LSTM to capture contextual dependencies from both past and future notes. The latent space was defined by $q_{\phi}(z|X)=\mathcal{N}(\mu(X),\sigma(X))$, sampled via the reparameterization trick:
\begin{equation}
z=\mu+\sigma\odot\epsilon, \quad \epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)
\end{equation}

The loss function optimizes the Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO):
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{VAE}=\mathcal{L}_{recon}+\beta D_{KL}(q_{\phi}(z|X)||p(z))
\end{equation}

To prevent posterior collapse---a common failure mode where the decoder ignores the latent code---we implemented a $\beta$-annealing schedule. $\beta$ was held at $0.0$ for the first 15 epochs to force the model to minimize reconstruction loss, before stepping to $\beta=0.001$.

\subsection{Task 3: Causal Music Transformer}
To overcome the vanishing gradient issues of LSTMs on long sequences, we implemented a Transformer decoder.

\textbf{Architecture details:} The model features an embedding dimension of 256, 4 Transformer blocks, 8 attention heads, and standard sine/cosine positional encoding. We generated a square subsequent mask to ensure strict causality, modeling the autoregressive probability:
\begin{equation}
p(X)=\prod_{t=1}^{T}p(x_{t}|x_{<t})
\end{equation}
The model was trained over 20 epochs using Cross Entropy loss:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{TR}=-\sum_{t=1}^{T}\log~p_{\theta}(x_{t}|x_{<t})
\end{equation}

\subsection{Task 4: RLHF Tuning}
Transformers often suffer from exposure bias and can drift into repetitive loops during inference. To correct this, we optimized the model using human feedback. 

First, we collected a dataset of generated samples scored by 10 human participants on a scale of 1 to 5. We trained an LSTM-based \textbf{Reward Model (RM)} to map a tokenized sequence $X_{gen}$ to a predicted human score $r$.

Next, we fine-tuned the Task 3 Transformer using the Policy Gradient algorithm to maximize the expected reward:
\begin{equation}
\max_{\theta}\mathbb{E}[r(X_{gen})]
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\nabla_{\theta}J(\theta)=\mathbb{E}[r\nabla_{\theta}\log~p_{\theta}(X)]
\end{equation}
We incorporated a baseline reward moving average to center the advantages and reduce gradient variance during training.



\section{Experimental Results and Evaluation}

\subsection{Training Dynamics and Convergence}
All models were trained utilizing PyTorch with CUDA acceleration.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Task 1 (LSTM AE):} Converged smoothly over 25 epochs. The BCE loss dropped from an initial 0.1016 to 0.0620, proving the model successfully learned sparse piano-roll matrices. Figure \ref{fig:bce_loss} illustrates the smooth exponential decay of the reconstruction loss over the training period, confirming stable convergence.
\end{itemize}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Task 2 (VAE):} Started with an ELBO loss of 997.91. Following the introduction of the KL-divergence penalty at epoch 16, the loss stabilized at 489.46, successfully learning a continuous multi-genre latent space.
    \item \textbf{Task 3 (Transformer):} Sequence length was set to 256 tokens. The model achieved an average loss of 2.1664 and an excellent final perplexity of \textbf{8.73}, indicating high predictive confidence.
    \item \textbf{Task 4 (Reward Model):} The Reward Model's MSE loss on human survey data dropped significantly from 39.66 to 0.033 over 20 epochs.
\end{itemize}

\section{Discussion}
The progression of models highlights the necessity of architectural complexity for musical tasks. Task 1 suffered from ``stuck note'' phenomena, where the autoregressive decoder would fall into repetitive loops without teacher forcing. Task 2 successfully generated diverse multi-instrument tracks, but lacked macro-structure (e.g., repeating a verse/chorus pattern). 

Task 3 solved the long-horizon issue, generating multi-minute tracks with clear phrasing. However, the true breakthrough occurred in Task 4. By applying Policy Gradients against the trained Reward Model, the generator learned to suppress dissonant token combinations and favor highly syncopated, human-pleasing rhythms, raising the subjective score to 4.8/5.0.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
In this study, we successfully developed a deep unsupervised learning pipeline capable of generating complex, multi-genre musical compositions. By transitioning from fixed-grid piano rolls to event-based tokenization, and leveraging both Self-Attention and Reinforcement Learning, we demonstrated that AI can be aligned with human musical aesthetics without explicit labeling. 

Future work will investigate the application of continuous Diffusion models to raw audio waveforms, moving beyond symbolic MIDI to synthesize entirely novel timbres and instruments.
